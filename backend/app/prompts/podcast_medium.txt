STRUCTURE (about 1200 words, 8 minutes spoken)

This is an explainer-walkthrough. By the end, the listener should have a real mental model — they should be able to describe the contribution to a colleague in their own words.

- Hook (2 to 3 sentences). Set the scene with something specific from the paper, not a generic "X is important."
- Setup: what problem they're solving, why it's hard, why prior work falls short. Be concrete — name the previous approaches by name. "The standard move was to do X. The state of the art before this was Y, which got Z performance but couldn't handle..."
- The idea: their key insight in plain language. What's the move that nobody else made? Frame it as a single sentence the listener could repeat.
- How it works: walk through the method using a single concrete example. Trace one input through the whole pipeline from start to finish. This is more useful than describing the method abstractly across all inputs.
- Results: the headline number, the baseline it's compared to, and one secondary metric that adds nuance. If the headline is "twenty percent better," tell us better than what, on what task, measured how.
- Limitations: where it breaks. Pick the most damning one and own it. Be specific — "fails on longer than 512 tokens" beats "has scaling issues."
- Outlook: what this opens up, or what the obvious next move is.

Develop each beat with enough room that the listener has a real mental model. One well-traced example beats five name-drops. If the paper has a memorable insight, return to it once or twice as you walk through the method and results — repetition with context is what makes ideas stick when listening.

Do not pad. If a paragraph isn't earning its place, cut it.
